APATURIA

article · 1,576 chars · lineage ↗ · page image at NLS ↗

APATURIA, in antiquity, a solemn feast celebrated by the Athenians in honour of Bacchus. The word is usually derived from apate, fraud. It is said to have been instituted in memory of a fraudulent victory obtained by Melanthus, king of Atheas, over Xanthus, king

Apaulia king of Boeotia, in a single combat, which they agreed upon, to put an end to a debate between them relating to the frontiers of their countries. Hence Budæus calls it festum deceptionis, "the feast of deceit."

Other authors give a different etymology of this feast: They tell us, that the young Athenians were not admitted into the tribes on the third day of the apaturia, till their fathers had first sworn that they were their own children; and that, till that time, they were supposed, in some measure, to be without fathers, απατροίς; whence the feast, say they, took its name. Xenophon, on the other hand, informs us, that the relations and friends met on this occasion, and joined with the fathers of the young people who were to be received into the tribes; and that from this assembly the feast took its name: that in απατροίς, the α, far from being a privative, being here a conjunctive, signifies the same thing with with together. This feast lasted four days: the first day, those of the same tribe made merry together; and this they called δῆριν. The second day, which they called απατροίς, they sacrificed to Jupiter and Minerva. The third day, which they called χελωτρίς, such of their young men and maids as were of age were admitted into their tribes. The fourth day they called επιδῆρις.
