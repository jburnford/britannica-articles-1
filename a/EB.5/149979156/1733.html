CREUSA, in fabulous history, daughter of Creon king of Corinth. As she was going to marry Jason, who had divorced Medea, she put on a poisoned garment, which immediately set her body on fire, and she expired in the most excruciating torments. She had received this gown as a gift from Medea, who wished to take that revenge upon the infidelity of Jason. Some call her Glaucé. (Ovid. de Art. Am. 1. v. 335.) A daughter of Priam, king of Troy by Hecuba. She married Aeneas, by whom she had, among other children, Ascanius. When Troy was taken, she fled in the night with her husband; but they were separated in the midst of the confusion and tumult, and Aeneas could not recover her, nor hear where she was. Some say that Cybele saved her, and carried her to her temple, of which she became priestess. Paus. 10. c. 26.—Virg. Æn. 2. v. 562.
