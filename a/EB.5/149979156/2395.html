DEFENDERS

DEFENDERS, were anciently notable dignitaries both in church and state, whose business was to look to the preservation of the public weal, to protect the poor and helpless, and to maintain the interests and causes of churches and religious houses. See PROTECTOR.—The council of Chalcedon, can. 2. calls the defender of a church Extores. Codin, de officiis aulae Const. makes mention of defenders of the palace. There were also a defender of the kingdom, defensor regni;

About the year 420, each patriarchal church began to have its defender; which custom was afterwards introduced into other churches, and continued to later days under other names; as those of Advocate, and Advocatus.

In the year 407, we find the council of Carthage asking the emperor for defenders, of the number of Scholastici, i. e. advocates who were in office; and that it might be allowed them to enter and search the cabinets and papers of the judges and other civil magistrates, whenever it should be found necessary for the interest of the church.
