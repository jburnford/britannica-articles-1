NAUPACTUS, or NAUPACTOM, (anc. geog.) a city of Ætolia, at the mouth of the Euenus. The word is derived from ΝΑΥΕ and ΠΑΚΤΟΣ, because it was there that the Heraclidae built the first ship which carried them to Peloponnesus. It first belonged to the Locri Ozolæ, and afterwards fell into the hands of the Athenians, who gave it to the Messenians, who

Nauplia had been driven from Peloponnesus by the Lacedemonians. It became the property of the Lacedemonians after the battle of Agolpotamos, and it was restored to the Locri. Philip of Macedonia afterwards took it, and gave it to the Etolians; from which circumstance it has generally been called one of the chief cities of their country. L. Long. 22. 20. N. Lat. 38. 0.

There was on the shore a temple of Neptune, and near it a cave filled with offerings, and dedicated to Venus, where widows resorted to request new husbands of the goddess. Pausan. lib. 10. p. 898.
