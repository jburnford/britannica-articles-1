LEONINE

article · 733 chars · lineage ↗ · page image at NLS ↗

LEONINE, in poetry, is applied to a kind of verses which rhyme at every hemistich, the middle always chiming to the end. Of which kind we find several ancient hymns, epigrams, prophecies, &c.—For instance, Muretus speaking of the poetry of Lorenzo Gambara of Bresse, says,

Brusia, vestratis merdusa volumina vatis,
Non sunt nostrates tergere digna nates.

The following one is from the school of Salernum:
Ut vites poenam de potibus inipe cenam.

The origin of the word is somewhat obscure: Palsquier derives it from one Leoninus or Leonius, who excelled in this way; and dedicated several pieces to Pope Alexander III.; others derive it from Pope Leo 3. and others from the beast called lion, by reason it is the loftiest of all verses.
