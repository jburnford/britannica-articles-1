FILTER

FILTER, or FILTRÆ, in chemistry, &c. a piece of woolen cloth, linen, paper, or other matter, some of which are in the form of hollow inverted cones, used to filtrate or strain liquors through. The filtre has the same use and effect with regard to liquids that the sieve or seaze has in dry matters.

Filters are of two sorts. The first are simple pieces of paper or cloth, through which the liquor is passed without farther trouble. The second are twisted up like a skin or wick, and first wetted, then squeezed, and one end put in the vessel that contains the liquor to be filtrated; the other end is to be out, and hang down below the surface of the liquor; by means hereof of the purest part of the liquor distils drop by drop out of the vessel, leaving the coarser part behind. This filter acts as a siphon.

FILTER is also a charm, supposed to have a virtue of inspiring love. The word is derived from φίλησις, which signifies the same thing, of φιλέω, amo "I love."

The Greeks, when their love was without success, had several arts to procure the affections of their beloved. The Thessalian women were famous for their skill in this as well as other magical practices. The means whereby it was effected were of divers sorts; it was sometimes done by potions called φίλησις, which are frequently mentioned in authors of both languages. Juvenal speaks thus:

Hic magis affert carnis, hic Thessala venit
Philtera, quibus valent uxores mariti.

Their operations were violent and dangerous, and commonly deprived such as drank them of their reason. Plutarch and Corneilius Nepos report, that Lucullus the Roman general first lost his reason, and afterwards his life, by one of them. Lucretius the poet ended his life by the same way; and Caius Caligula, as Suetonius reports, was driven into a fit of madness by a fil-
