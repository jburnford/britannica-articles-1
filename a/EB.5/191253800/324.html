SEVERANCE

SEVERANCE, in law, the singling or severing two or more that join or are joined in the same writ or action. As if two join in a writ, de legitime probanda, and the one be afterwards nonsuited; here severance is

VOL. XVII. Part I.

permitted, so as notwithstanding the nonsuit the one, the other may severally proceed.

There is also severance of the tenants in assize; when one, two, or more disseisees appear upon the writ, and not the other. And severance in debt, where two executors are named plaintiffs, and the one refuses to prosecute. We also meet with severance of summonses, severance in attaints, &c. An estate in joint tenancy may be severed and destroyed by destroying any of its units. 1. That of time, which respects only the original commencement of the joint estate, cannot indeed (being now part) be affected by any subsequent transaction. But, 2. The joint-tenants estate may be destroyed without any alienation, by merely disuniting their possession. 3. The jointure may be destroyed, by destroying the unity of title. And, 4. By destroying the unity of interest.
