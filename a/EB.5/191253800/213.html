SEPTENNIAL Elections. Blackstone, in his Commentaries, Vol. I. p. 189. says, (after observing that the utmost extent of time allowed the same parliament to sit by the stat. 6 W. and M. c. 2. was three years), "But, by the statute 1 Geo. I. fl. 2. c. 38. (in order proffessedly to prevent the great and continued expences of frequent elections, and the violent heats and animosities consequent thereupon, and for the peace and security of the government, just then recovering from the late rebellion), this term was prolonged to seven years; and what alone is an instance of the vast authority of parliament, the very same house that was chosen for three years enacted its own continuance for seven."
