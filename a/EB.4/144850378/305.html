MISE

article · 1,070 chars · lineage ↗ · page image at NLS ↗

MISE, in law-books, is used in various senses: thus it sometimes signifies costs or expences, in which sense it is commonly used in entering of judgments in actions personal. It is also used for the issue to be tried on the grand assize; in which case, joining of the mise upon the mere right, is putting in issue between the tenant and demandant, who has the best or clearest right.

MISE also signifies a tax or tollage, &c. An honorary gift, or customary present from the people of Wales to every new king or prince of Wales, anciently given in cattle, wine, and corn, but now in money, being 5000l. or more, is denominated a mise: so was the usual tribute or fine of 3000 marks paid by the inhabitants of the county palatine of Chester, at the change of every owner of the said earldoms, for enjoying their liberties. And at Chester they have a mise-book, wherein every town and village in the county is rated what to pay towards the mise. The 27 Hen. VIII. c. 26. ordains that lords shall have all such mises and profits of their lands as they had in times past, &c.
